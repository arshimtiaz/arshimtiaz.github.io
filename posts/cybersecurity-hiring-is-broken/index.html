Cybersecurity Is Changing — The Industry Isn’t

Let me just say it straight: the way the cybersecurity world operates today feels painfully outdated.

Teenagers with a half-decent laptop, curiosity, and a bit of Reddit can hack into Fortune 500 companies — and no, that’s not just a movie plot. It’s real. It’s happening. And it makes you wonder… if that’s the reality, why does it still feel so hard for passionate, capable people to break into this industry?

What I’m about to say isn’t coming from frustration alone — it’s from a place of genuine care for the field. I’m a fresh graduate, and I want to be part of making this space better. I haven’t been around long enough to be jaded, and maybe that’s the point. Sometimes it takes new eyes to question what’s become normalized.

The High School Hacker vs. The Certified Professional

We’re still stuck in a world where hiring managers filter resumes for alphabet soup — CEH, CISSP, OSCP — as if those letters alone unlock security wisdom.

Meanwhile, there’s a teenager somewhere reverse engineering malware after school — just for fun. Guess who’s making the real impact?

Certifications can show dedication, no doubt. But when they become gatekeepers instead of guidelines, we risk overlooking truly talented people who don’t “fit the mold” — but could’ve been the exact minds we needed.

We Talk About Cyber Like It’s Doomsday — Then Hesitate to Hire People Who Care

Every week, headlines scream about ransomware shutting down hospitals or customer data being leaked. Executives talk about “cyber resilience” and “zero trust” like they’re the new gospel.

But when it comes to actually hiring passionate, skilled newcomers?

- “We’re looking for someone with 7 years of experience.”
- “Do you have your CISSP yet?”
- “We’ll get back to you.” (And usually… they don’t.)

It’s strange how quickly some companies will pour money into shiny tools — but hesitate to invest in the people who will configure, defend, and truly understand them.

Certifications Don’t Equal Competence

Security isn’t something you just follow from a checklist. It’s unpredictable, messy, and constantly evolving.

And yet, so many job listings feel like they were written in 2008:

Must have 5+ years of experience in a tool that’s 2 years old
Must hold 3 certifications
Must have experience mitigating APT-level threats (in a junior role, no less)

It leaves you thinking… are we hiring for potential, or just trying to tick boxes?

The Real Threat Isn’t Just Hackers — It’s Complacency

Honestly, the scariest part of cybersecurity right now isn’t the external threats — it’s the internal mindset that security is “just another department.”

When cybersecurity is treated like a checklist or a part-time concern, it only becomes a priority after something goes wrong.

Like quitting smoking only after being diagnosed with cancer.
Until then, we ignore the warnings and hope for the best.

Too often, companies “do enough” to meet compliance — then move on. But compliance isn’t the same as being secure.

“A Service When Needed” — The Risk of Risk Acceptance

Sometimes it feels like cybersecurity is treated more like a service than a strategy.

Here’s a common pattern:

  1. A company decides it’s time to “get serious” about security.
  2. They bring in a third-party firm for an audit.
  3. The report comes back with a long list of recommendations.
  4. Only the most critical issues get addressed — the rest are marked “risk accepted.”

Risk acceptance can be valid in some contexts. But over time, this culture of minimalism lets small issues become vulnerabilities. Having a capable internal cyber team — empowered to fix everything, not just the criticals — can make all the difference.

What Cybersecurity Actually Needs

We need more people who are curious, creative, and not afraid to ask:

“What if we tried it this way instead?”

Security isn’t a plug-and-play situation. Threats differ. Contexts vary. The idea that a single framework fits every company, every breach, every team… it just doesn’t hold up.

We need organizations that don’t just put “innovation” in their values — but actually give space for their teams to think differently and experiment.

Let the Passionate and the Weirdos In

Some of the most brilliant cybersecurity minds I’ve come across didn’t finish school, didn’t have fancy titles, and didn’t follow the “right path.”

They just lived security. They broke stuff (ethically). Built tools. Learned because they loved it.

If hiring filters them out because of resume formatting or missing acronyms, that’s not a talent problem — that’s a hiring problem.

Security Isn’t a 9-to-5

For many of us, it’s not just a job — it’s a mindset. A craft. Something we care deeply about.

So let the passionate ones in. Let the outliers have a voice. Let teams personalize their approach based on real threats and real scenarios — not just outdated templates and roadblocks.

Because if we keep hiring and thinking like it’s 2004, we’re setting ourselves up to lose — badly.

That’s the rant. That’s the reality. If this rubbed you the wrong way, maybe that’s a conversation worth having.
